The problem

What credit card fraud actually looks like

Fraud rarely looks like a single "bad" transaction — it's a pattern across amount, device, location and timing that deviates from how a genuine cardholder behaves. Here are the patterns CheckFraud is built to recognize.

🔐

Card-not-present fraud

Stolen card numbers used online or over the phone, without the physical card ever being swiped or tapped.

📱

Phishing & social engineering

Victims are tricked into revealing card details, OTPs, or login credentials through fake messages or calls.

💳

Card skimming

Card data is copied at compromised ATMs or POS terminals, then cloned onto blank cards or reused digitally.

👤

Identity theft

A fraudster opens or takes over an account using stolen personal information rather than card data directly.

🔑

Account takeover

Attackers gain access to an existing account — often via leaked passwords — and transact as the real owner.

🌐

Geo & velocity anomalies

Transactions from an unusual location, or an implausible number of purchases in a short time window.

Stay protected

Ten ways to prevent card fraud

Detection models are the last line of defense. Good habits are the first — these are the practices CheckFraud's risk engine effectively rewards with a lower score.

01
Enable transaction alerts

Get an SMS or push notification for every charge so you spot anything unfamiliar within seconds.

02
Turn on two-factor authentication

Require an OTP or biometric check for logins and payments beyond a certain amount.

03
Avoid public Wi-Fi for payments

Use mobile data or a trusted network when entering card details or completing checkout.

04
Use virtual card numbers

Many banks offer disposable card numbers for online shopping so your real number is never exposed.

05
Check ATMs and terminals

Look for loose card slots, hidden cameras, or unusual attachments before inserting your card.

06
Never share OTPs or PINs

No legitimate bank will ever call and ask you to read out a one-time password.

07
Review statements monthly

Small, recurring "test" charges are a common early sign your card details have leaked.

08
Freeze your card instantly

Use your banking app to freeze a lost or suspicious card immediately, rather than waiting on a call center.

09
Watch for phishing links

Verify sender domains before clicking links that ask you to "verify" your card or account.

10
Set a low default transaction limit

Raise it only when you need to make a large purchase, shrinking your exposure by default.

💡 Why Random Forest, and an honest caveat

Random Forest is the model actually saved to disk (credit_card_fraud_model.pkl, paired with scaler.pkl), and it edges out the others on accuracy and precision. But recall sits at just 7% — it misses most fraud cases when the decision threshold is the default 0.5. That's a real, disclosed limitation: the 14 raw columns in this dataset (amount, bank, state, category) carry only a weak statistical relationship with the IsFraud label, which is common in compact, anonymized fraud datasets built for learning rather than production. The Prediction demo on this site intentionally asks for richer behavioral and device signals — OTP verification, device trust, login attempts, geo-mismatch — because those are the features that move recall in real fraud systems, and they're the natural next step beyond this dataset.
